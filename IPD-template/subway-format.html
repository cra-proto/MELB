Subway (multi-page) format

The subway pattern is a multi-page navigation structure that guides users through a series of related steps. It uses a vertical progress indicator (resembling a subway or metro line) to show users where they are in the process.

When to use the subway pattern

Conditions for using the subway pattern
ConditionExample
The process has clearly distinct phases that users may complete at different times or sessionsApplying for the Disability Tax Credit
Each phase has enough content to warrant its own pageEligibility criteria alone fill a full page with decision trees
Users need to understand one phase before they can act on the nextYou must confirm eligibility before you apply
Different users may enter the process at different stagesSomeone who has already applied comes back to check “After you apply”
The content serves a major benefit, credit, or service with high user volumeCanada Dental Benefit, Canada Workers Benefit

Indicators the subway pattern is needed

  • A single page would exceed 8–10 steps and multiple substantial sections
  • The content naturally breaks into 3–8 distinct phases
  • Users would benefit from seeing their progress through the process
  • Usability testing or analytics show users are struggling with a long single page
  • The content includes complex eligibility, detailed preparation requirements, and post-submission follow-up

Components of a subway

Index page (required)
A landing page that introduces the service and lists all steps with short doormat descriptions. Uses a definition list for the navigation.
Step pages (required)
Individual pages for each step in the process. Each has a double heading: the service name appears as a small label above the step-specific title. Navigation is forward-only.
Sub-pages (optional)
Supporting pages that branch off from a step for detailed or supplementary content. Use sparingly.

Subway pattern rules

  1. Limit to 3–8 steps

    Fewer than 3 does not justify the pattern. More than 8 overwhelms users.

  2. Use forward-only navigation

    Step pages should only have a “Next” pagination button. Users can use the subway nav sidebar or the browser back button.

  3. Always include an index page

    The index page introduces the service and lists all steps with doormat descriptions.

  4. Use the double heading pattern

    Step pages display the service name as a smaller label above the step-specific H1.

  5. Keep step titles short and action-oriented

    Use the recommended heading patterns: “Who can apply”, “How to apply”, “After you apply”. See Recommended heading patterns.

  6. Use sub-pages sparingly

    Only add sub-pages when a step has supplementary content that would make the step page too long.

  7. Make each step self-contained

    A user arriving at any step page should understand what that step covers without reading the previous steps.
