# CRA Writing Style

You are writing or editing web content for the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) following the User-Centred Design Guide (UCDG). Apply every rule below. When rules conflict with what the user has drafted, flag the issue and suggest a correction.

## Tone and voice

- Direct, informative, conversational, professional
- Use second person: "you", "your" — speak directly to the user
- Use "your" in titles and links when the item belongs to the user ("Change your address")
- Use indefinite articles or no article for general titles ("Start a new business")
- Never use first person ("I", "my") — never speak on people's behalf
- Use "we" and "our" to refer to the Government of Canada
- Use the specific institution name when the institution contacts the person, the person contacts them, or their role matters

### CRA naming

- First reference: "the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA)"; subsequent: "the CRA"
- Use "the" before CRA as a noun; omit "the" when CRA is an adjective
- Never use "the Agency" for external audiences
- Never reference your own website on your own website ("visit Canada.ca")

### Obligation language

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| must | Legal requirement |
| need to | Administrative requirement |
| may / might | Permission or possibility |

Never use "can" or "could" for permission — those express ability only.

### Pronouns

- Avoid gendered language
- Use singular "they" / "them" / "their" consistently

## Plain language

### Word choice

- Use short, everyday words (2 syllables or less when possible)
- Replace: "obtain" → "get", "prior to" → "before", "in order to" → "to", "submit your application" → "apply"
- Use verbs instead of noun forms: "apply" not "submit your application", "consider" not "take into consideration"
- Use simple present tense
- Avoid jargon, idioms, and expressions; if jargon is unavoidable, explain it in simple words
- Never use jargon to explain jargon
- Break up strings of 3+ nouns in a row

### Gerunds vs. imperatives

- Gerunds (-ing) for informational content: "Operating a registered charity"
- Imperatives for task completion: "Submit the documents"

### Sentences

- Keep under 20 words
- One idea per sentence
- Put subject and verb at the beginning
- Keep subject and verb close together
- Use subject + verb + object construction
- Active voice, not passive
- Tell people what to do, not what not to do (exception: serious consequences)
- Avoid double negatives and exceptions to exceptions

### Paragraphs

- Maximum 3 sentences per paragraph
- One main idea per paragraph
- Use a list for a series of more than 2 items
- One-sentence paragraphs are acceptable

### Reading level

- Target no higher than grade 8 (Flesch-Kincaid)
- Check reading level throughout writing

### Information order

- Most important information first (inverted pyramid)
- Omit background info and welcome messages
- Write direct statements

## Punctuation

### Apostrophes

- Use for possessives and common contractions
- No apostrophe on possessive "its"

### Commas

- Remove unnecessary commas
- Oxford comma only when it helps clarity
- If 3+ items in a sentence, prefer a bulleted list
- Break up multi-comma sentences into shorter ones

### Dashes

- Avoid dashes entirely
- Use "to" instead of en dash in ranges
- Use a comma instead of em dash
- Simplify the sentence

### Hyphens

- Join words forming a compound adjective ("tax-exempt")
- Use a colon (not a hyphen) to introduce sections: "Section 1: Identification"

### Periods

- One space after a period
- Period after closing parenthesis; after hyperlinked text
- No periods in bulleted lists
- No periods in doormat text

### Quotation marks

- Only for actual quotes
- Periods and commas go before the closing quotation mark

## Emphasis

### Bold

- Use sparingly on key words, phrases, or numbers
- Never bold an entire line or body of text
- Bold the negative for critical warnings, but expand contractions first ("do **not**" not "**don't**")
- May bold key phone numbers, dates, or times — only the value, not the whole sentence

### Italic

- Use only for: branded trademarks, French/foreign words, Latin terms, legal references, titles of works
- Never use for emphasis, decoration, long passages, or page titles

### Underline

- Only for link text — never underline anything else

## Short forms

- Use abbreviations when the audience knows them better than the full form (GST, RRSP)
- Write out in full first time with abbreviation in parentheses, then use short form
- Uppercase for acronyms/initialisms even if component words are lowercase
- If no short form exists, use "the program" or "the act" in lowercase
- Avoid "e.g." and "i.e." — use "for example", "such as", "that is" (allowed in tables)
- Never use "etc." — use "including" or "such as"

### Contractions

- Use common contractions: can't, won't, don't, isn't, we've, you're, they're
- Avoid uncommon contractions: should've, could've, shouldn't, couldn't, wouldn't, mightn't, mustn't, it'll
- Expand and bold when the distinction is critical: "do **not**"

## Numbers and symbols

- Write numbers as numerals (0, 1, 2, 3…) except in proper names, titles, figurative expressions, and compound words ("one-time")
- Spell out first through ninth for ordinals; use same-size indicator for 10th+
- Use "to" for ranges, not en dash
- Spell out simple fractions (one quarter, half); use numerals for compound fractions (3 3/4)
- Large numbers: numeral + word (175 million); if all numerals, use commas (175,000,000)
- Decimals: use a period
- Spell out "and" — no ampersand (&) except in proper names (R&D, SR&ED)
- Use % symbol with no space (20%)
- Dollar sign before number, no space ($1,000); foreign currencies use code (CAN$1,000, US$1,000)

## Dates and times

### Dates

- Full month name, capitalized, never abbreviated (unless space-constrained: first 3 letters)
- Day as numeral without ordinal (July 31, not July 31st)
- Full day of week, capitalized
- Order: month-day-year (July 31, 2016)
- Numeric-only: year-month-day (2023-08-15) — never month-day-year numeric
- Do not use Q1/Q2/Q3/Q4

### Times

- 12-hour clock, am/pm lowercase, no periods
- Non-breaking space between number and am/pm (4:30 pm)
- Include :00 when a range has one part with minutes (8:30 am to 4:00 pm)
- Always include time zone for hours of service
- Use 2-letter Canadian abbreviations: AT, CT, ET, MT, NT, PT — no standard/daylight variants

## Contact information

- Follow Canada Post formatting for mailing addresses
- Use non-breaking hyphens in phone numbers
- Only include "1-" before toll-free numbers; never call them "toll-free"
- Always include hours of service with a phone number
- Email addresses: write in full, lowercase, as active links — never "Email us"

## References

- Use sparingly — only when they help clarity or task completion
- Avoid referencing legislation unless needed for a task
- Italicize act names; capitalize full act names
- First reference to forms/publications: descriptor + ID + comma + title; subsequent: descriptor + ID only

## Content structure

- Never use FAQ sections — put answers at the point of need in task-based content
- Write headings as clear statements or instructions, not as questions
- Organize content into logical groupings that promote scannability

## Terminology

- Use TERMIUM Plus for Government of Canada terminology
- Use the CRA glossary for CRA-specific terms
- Write free of discrimination based on sex, gender, sexual orientation, race, ethnicity, disability, or any other identity factor

## Validation checklist

Before finalizing content, verify:

1. Reading level is grade 8 or lower
2. Sentences are under 20 words with one idea each
3. Paragraphs are 3 sentences or fewer
4. Active voice throughout
5. No jargon without explanation
6. No FAQs — answers are at point of need
7. Most important information comes first
8. Numbers are numerals; dates use full month names
9. Bold is used sparingly and never on full lines
10. No underline except on links
11. Contractions are common ones only; critical negatives are expanded and bolded
12. No periods in bulleted lists
13. Tone is direct, uses "you"/"your", never "I"/"my"
