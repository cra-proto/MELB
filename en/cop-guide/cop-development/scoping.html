Scoping process – How a COP is developed

Scoping determines which Canada.ca pages will be optimized in your COP. The GST/HST Business COP's scoped inventory page reveals exactly how experienced designers approach this critical first step.

The scoping classification system

Every Canada.ca page in the topic area is listed in the repo's index.html and tagged with a confidence label:

Scoping confidence labels
Label Meaning Visual indicator
Definitely This page is in scope – will be optimized Blue
Probably Likely in scope – may be optimized Green
Unlikely Probably out of scope – won't be changed Yellow
(no label) Out of scope – included for context only None

Why use confidence labels instead of yes/no? Experienced designers tag pages with confidence levels precisely because they know scope will shift during co-design and SME review. The three-level system communicates uncertainty honestly and sets expectations with stakeholders.

How the scoped inventory is organized

The inventory mirrors the Canada.ca URL structure, organized by path. In the GST/HST COP:

  1. digital-services-businesses/ – 30 pages across 4 filing method silos (mostly tagged "Definitely")
  2. topics/gst-hst-businesses/ – 10 topic-level pages plus 6 sub-pages under the filing return subway
  3. Context pages without labels – Account management (register, change, close), charge/collect pages, rebates, definitions

This approach ensures the team has visibility into the full content landscape, even for pages they don't plan to change. Context pages help designers understand adjacent content that may be affected by their restructuring decisions.

What the scoping decisions look like

In the GST/HST COP with 66 pages in scope:

  • In scope (Definitely/Probably): All filing method pages across both sections, plus the return-filing subway, penalties, and payment timing pages
  • Out of scope: Account management pages (register, change, close), charge/collect pages, rebates, definitions – adjacent content that doesn't need optimization in this COP
  • Interesting edge case: The need-access-code.html page was tagged "Unlikely" initially but ended up being modified and moved (it became get-gst-hst-access-code.html under the new how-file section) – scoping evolves during the project

How scope evolves during a COP

COPs almost always grow beyond their initial scope:

Scope evolution across COPs
COP Initial scope Final scope What was added mid-project
EOL 6 pages 50+ pages Notify-death page, entire income types section, clearance certificate expansion, credits/deductions section
CAP 8 pages 120+ pages Corporate instalments (12 pages), debt collections (7 pages), unable-to-pay (3 pages), CRA future IA exploration
GST/HST 66 pages 33 pages (net) GST relief emergency page, calculate-prepare-report hub, accessibility test pages
TFSA ~15 pages 35+ pages Payment options, permitted investments, excess contributions splits, definitions system

Note: The GST/HST COP is unusual in that it reduced the total page count (66 → 33) by consolidating redundant content. Most COPs expand as the team discovers gaps and adds missing content. Plan for scope growth in your timeline.

What drives scope changes

Four consistent drivers of scope change appear across all COPs:

User testing reveals gaps

The EOL COP's notify-death page was added because testing showed people needed a "tell CRA someone died" starting point. This page didn't exist on Canada.ca at all – the team discovered the gap when participants couldn't find it.

SME review adds complexity

The TFSA COP's excess contributions section split from one page into up to 4 pages after subject matter experts said the single page couldn't adequately cover the nuances of different excess contribution scenarios.

External events force additions

The GST/HST COP's GST relief page was an emergency insertion built in 4 days (Nov 2024), driven by a government policy announcement. COP repos sometimes absorb urgent unplanned work that falls within the content area.

IA redesign creates new structural needs

When the GST/HST COP consolidated 28 silo pages into method-specific pages, the team realized they needed a new "calculate and prepare" hub that didn't exist before. Restructuring the IA revealed gaps that the old structure had hidden.

The CHANGELOG as a scope tracking tool

The CHANGELOG.md file is both a scope tracker during the project and the production handoff deliverable at the end. It contains three tables:

CHANGELOG table structure
Table Columns Purpose
Pages to be added Page title, GitHub prototype URL, New Canada.ca URL Track brand new pages that need to be created on Canada.ca
Pages to be deleted Page title, Current Canada.ca URL, Redirect target URL Track pages to remove, with where their traffic should redirect
Pages to be modified Old title, Old URL, New title, New URL, Date modified update needed? Track pages that are changing title, URL, or content

In the GST/HST COP, the CHANGELOG tracked: 3 pages added, 36 pages deleted, 22 pages modified – with exact old and new URLs for every single page.

Redirect planning

Every deleted page needs a redirect target – you cannot simply remove a page from Canada.ca without telling the system where to send existing traffic and bookmarks.

Redirect planning happens in the CHANGELOG's "Pages to be deleted" table. For each page being removed, the designer specifies which new or existing page should receive the redirect.

Good redirect planning follows these principles:

  • Redirect to the closest equivalent content – if a NETFILE FAQ page is deleted, redirect to the new NETFILE filing method page that absorbed the FAQ content
  • Never redirect to a page that is also being deleted – this creates redirect chains
  • Consider the user's intent – someone who bookmarked the "NETFILE eligibility" page wants to know if they can use NETFILE, so redirect to the page that answers that question
  • Document every redirect – the web publishing team needs this to configure the server-side redirects
