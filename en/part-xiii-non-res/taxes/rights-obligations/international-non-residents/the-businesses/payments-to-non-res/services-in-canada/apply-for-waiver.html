Waiver for contractors

A non-resident who provides services in Canada can apply to reduce or eliminate the 15% Regulation 105 withholding tax. To apply, the non-resident must complete Form R105, Regulation 105 Waiver Application.

The CRA reviews the application and, if approved, sends a letter to both the non-resident and the payer confirming the reduced withholding rate.

Warning You must not apply a reduced withholding rate unless you receive written authorization from the CRA.

When to submit

Submit the waiver application at least 30 days before the services begin in Canada. The CRA aims to process applications within 30 business days.

What to include

The R105 application must include clear, concise information and documentation to support the waiver request, including:

  • the non-resident's tax identification number (see How to submit your application for ID number requirements)
  • the nature of the services to be provided in Canada
  • the dates and duration of the work in Canada
  • the amount of the contract or expected payment
  • estimated Canadian-source expenses
  • the applicable tax treaty article, if requesting a treaty-based waiver

A waiver is specific to a particular contract or engagement. If the non-resident has multiple contracts, they must apply separately for each one.

Secondary withholding obligations

Even if the CRA approves a waiver, the non-resident is not relieved of their own obligation to withhold and remit tax on payments they make to sub-contractors or employees for services provided in Canada.

Treaty-based waivers for contractors

If the non-resident is a resident of a country that has a tax treaty with Canada, they may qualify for a waiver based on the treaty provisions. The most common grounds are that the non-resident does not have a permanent establishment or fixed base in Canada.

Waiver based on no permanent establishment

Many tax treaties allow a non-resident business to earn income in Canada without paying Canadian tax, as long as the business does not have a permanent establishment in Canada. A permanent establishment is generally a fixed place of business, such as an office, branch, or factory.

If the non-resident can show they do not have a permanent establishment in Canada, the CRA may approve a waiver of the Regulation 105 withholding tax.

Waiver based on no fixed base

Some tax treaties use the concept of a "fixed base" for individuals providing independent personal services. If the non-resident individual does not have a fixed base regularly available to them in Canada, the treaty may exempt their income from Canadian tax. In this case, the CRA may approve a waiver.

CRA waiver guidelines (Tests A, B, and C)

The CRA uses the following tests to evaluate treaty-based waiver requests from non-residents providing non-employment services in Canada:

CRA waiver tests for treaty-based Regulation 105 waivers
Criteria Test A Test B Test C
Who it applies to Individuals earning less than CAN$5,000 in the calendar year Individuals or corporations (not recurring) Individuals or corporations (recurring presence)
Days in current contract less than 180 N/A Yes Yes
Cumulative days less than 240 N/A N/A Yes
Waiver determination Waive withholding Waive unless exception applies Waive unless exception applies

If the non-resident does not meet any of these tests, the CRA will generally not approve a treaty-based waiver.

Simplified waiver for artists and athletes

The CRA offers a simplified waiver process for self-employed non-resident artists and athletes earning money for performances in Canada.

You may use the simplified process if the non-resident:

  • is a self-employed artist or athlete
  • is a resident of a country that has a tax treaty with Canada
  • will earn no more than CAN$15,000 during the calendar year for performances in Canada

Under the simplified process, the payer can reduce the withholding to the treaty rate (which may be zero) without waiting for prior CRA approval. The non-resident must still complete a waiver application, but the process is faster and requires less documentation.

Film and video acting services

A non-resident actor who receives payments for acting in a film or video production in Canada can apply for a reduction in Part XIII non-resident tax.

To apply, the non-resident must complete one of the following forms:

  • Form T1287, Application by a Non-Resident of Canada (Individual) — for individuals
  • Form T1288, Application by a Non-Resident of Canada (Corporation) — for corporations

When the CRA processes these forms, it sends a letter to the non-resident and the Canadian payer stating which payments qualify for a tax reduction. The reduction is based on the non-resident's estimated Canadian expenses related to earning the income.

Exemption for U.S.-resident actors

A non-resident actor who lives in the United States and earns less than $15,000 CAD from acting services in Canada in a calendar year may qualify for an exemption under Article XVI of the Canada–United States Tax Convention. They can apply for this exemption using Form T1287 or T1288.

Warning You must not apply a tax reduction unless you receive written authorization from the CRA. If you do receive authorization, report the amounts on an NR4 slip using exemption code "J".

Treaty-based waiver for employees

If the non-resident is an employee (not a contractor), they may qualify for a waiver of Regulation 102 withholding based on a tax treaty. This allows the employer to reduce payroll deductions for that employee.

To apply, the non-resident employee must complete Form R102-R, Regulation 102 Waiver Application.

The employee must show that they meet the conditions for exemption under the applicable tax treaty, which typically require that:

  • the employee is present in Canada for fewer than 183 days in the relevant period
  • the employer is not a resident of Canada and does not have a permanent establishment in Canada
  • the employee's pay is not borne by a permanent establishment in Canada

Payee certificate for permanent establishments

A non-resident who carries on business in Canada through a permanent establishment may apply for a Regulation 805.1 payee certificate. This certificate provides relief from Part XIII withholding tax on certain payments (such as interest, dividends, and royalties) that are attributable to the non-resident's business in Canada.

Under Regulation 805, amounts that can reasonably be attributed to business carried on through a permanent establishment in Canada are already exempt from Part XIII tax. The payee certificate serves as written confirmation from the CRA, which makes it easier for the payer to apply the exemption.

To apply, submit a written request to the International Tax Services Office that includes:

  • details about the permanent establishment in Canada
  • the nature and amount of the payments
  • evidence that the payments are attributable to the permanent establishment

How to submit your application

Online submission

You and your representatives can submit waiver applications (R105, R102-R, RC473) online through My Account, Represent a Client, or My Business Account.

By mail

Send completed waiver and certification applications to:

International Tax Services Office
Canada Revenue Agency
Post Office Box 9769, Station T
Ottawa ON  K1G 3Y4
Canada

Identification numbers you need

You need a Canadian tax identification number to complete waiver forms.

Identification numbers required for waiver applications
Applicant type ID number needed How to get one
Corporation Business Number (BN) Complete Form RC1 or attach it to your waiver application
Individual (with SIN) Social Insurance Number (SIN) Use your existing SIN
Individual (without SIN) Individual Tax Number (ITN) Apply using Form T1261, and attach it to your waiver application

To avoid delays, apply for your identification number before submitting your waiver application. If you cannot get the number in time, attach the ID number application form to the back of your waiver application.
