Withholding tax on services provided in Canada

Apply for a waiver or reduction

If the standard Regulation 105 or Regulation 102 withholding rate is more than the actual tax the non-resident will owe, you can ask the CRA in advance to lower or waive the withholding.

You can only withhold at the reduced rate after you receive written authorization from the CRA.

Steps to apply for a waiver or reduction

  1. Find out who can apply

    Match your situation to the right type of waiver or reduction, and the form you will need.

    Waiver and reduction types by situation
    Your situation Form to use
    Non-resident contractor providing services in Canada R105 — Regulation 105 Waiver Application
    Non-resident contractor with tax-treaty grounds (no permanent establishment, no fixed base, or meeting Tests A, B, or C) R105 (treaty-based)
    Self-employed non-resident artist or athlete earning no more than CAN$15,000 in the calendar year Simplified waiver process
    Non-resident individual actor in a film or video production T1287 — Application by a Non-Resident of Canada (Individual)
    Non-resident corporation providing acting services in a film or video production T1288 — Application by a Non-Resident of Canada (Corporation)
    Non-resident employee R102-R — Regulation 102 Waiver Application
    Non-resident carrying on business in Canada through a permanent establishment Regulation 805.1 payee certificate

  2. Choose your form

    Each waiver or reduction scenario uses a different CRA form. Select the one that matches your situation.

    R105 — Regulation 105 waiver (contractors)

    A non-resident providing services in Canada can apply to reduce or eliminate the 15% Regulation 105 withholding tax by completing Form R105, Regulation 105 Waiver Application. A waiver is specific to a single contract or engagement — if the non-resident has multiple contracts, they must apply separately for each one.

    Treaty-based grounds for an R105 waiver

    If the non-resident is a resident of a country that has a tax treaty with Canada, they may qualify for a waiver based on the treaty provisions. The most common grounds are:

    • No permanent establishment. Many tax treaties allow a non-resident business to earn income in Canada without paying Canadian tax, as long as the business does not have a permanent establishment (a fixed place of business such as an office, branch, or factory) in Canada.
    • No fixed base. Some tax treaties use the concept of a "fixed base" for individuals providing independent personal services. If the non-resident individual does not have a fixed base regularly available to them in Canada, the treaty may exempt their income from Canadian tax.
    CRA waiver guidelines (Tests A, B, and C)

    The CRA uses the following tests to evaluate treaty-based waiver requests from non-residents providing non-employment services in Canada:

    CRA waiver tests for treaty-based Regulation 105 waivers
    Criteria Test A Test B Test C
    Who it applies to Individuals earning less than CAN$5,000 in the calendar year Individuals or corporations (not recurring) Individuals or corporations (recurring presence)
    Days in current contract less than 180 N/A Yes Yes
    Cumulative days less than 240 N/A N/A Yes
    Waiver determination Waive withholding Waive unless exception applies Waive unless exception applies

    If the non-resident does not meet any of these tests, the CRA will generally not approve a treaty-based waiver.

    Simplified waiver for artists and athletes

    The CRA offers a simplified process for self-employed non-resident artists and athletes performing in Canada. You may use it if the non-resident:

    • is a self-employed artist or athlete
    • is a resident of a country that has a tax treaty with Canada
    • will earn no more than CAN$15,000 during the calendar year for performances in Canada

    Under the simplified process, the payer can reduce the withholding to the treaty rate (which may be zero) without waiting for prior CRA approval. The non-resident must still complete a waiver application, but the process is faster and requires less documentation.

    T1287 / T1288 — Tax reduction for non-resident actors

    A non-resident actor who receives payments for acting in a film or video production in Canada can apply for a reduction in Part XIII non-resident tax by completing:

    • Form T1287, Application by a Non-Resident of Canada (Individual) — for individuals
    • Form T1288, Application by a Non-Resident of Canada (Corporation) — for corporations

    When the CRA processes these forms, it sends a letter to the non-resident and the Canadian payer stating which payments qualify for a tax reduction. The reduction is based on the non-resident's estimated Canadian expenses related to earning the income.

    Exemption for U.S.-resident actors

    A non-resident actor who lives in the United States and earns less than $15,000 CAD from acting services in Canada in a calendar year may qualify for an exemption under Article XVI of the Canada–United States Tax Convention. They can apply for this exemption using Form T1287 or T1288.

    R102-R — Regulation 102 waiver (employees)

    If the non-resident is an employee (not a contractor), they may qualify for a waiver of Regulation 102 withholding based on a tax treaty. To apply, the employee must complete Form R102-R, Regulation 102 Waiver Application. The employee must show that they meet the treaty exemption conditions, which typically require that:

    • the employee is present in Canada for fewer than 183 days in the relevant period
    • the employer is not a resident of Canada and does not have a permanent establishment in Canada
    • the employee's pay is not borne by a permanent establishment in Canada

    Regulation 805.1 payee certificate (permanent establishment)

    A non-resident who carries on business in Canada through a permanent establishment may apply for a Regulation 805.1 payee certificate. This provides relief from Part XIII withholding tax on certain payments (such as interest, dividends, and royalties) that are attributable to the non-resident's business in Canada.

    Under Regulation 805, amounts that can reasonably be attributed to business carried on through a permanent establishment in Canada are already exempt from Part XIII tax. The payee certificate is the CRA's written confirmation, which makes it easier for the payer to apply the exemption. Submission for this certificate is by written request rather than a numbered form.

  3. Gather your supporting documents

    Every waiver application needs a Canadian tax identification number, plus form-specific evidence.

    Tax identification numbers

    You need one of the following identification numbers to complete a waiver form.

    Identification numbers required for waiver applications
    Applicant type ID number needed How to get one
    Corporation Business Number (BN) Complete Form RC1 or attach it to your waiver application
    Individual (with SIN) Social Insurance Number (SIN) Use your existing SIN
    Individual (without SIN) Individual Tax Number (ITN) Apply using Form T1261, and attach it to your waiver application

    To avoid delays, apply for your identification number before submitting your waiver application. If you cannot get the number in time, attach the ID number application form to the back of your waiver application.

    Form-specific evidence

    • R105: the nature of the services, dates and duration of the work in Canada, the contract amount or expected payment, estimated Canadian-source expenses, and (if applying for a treaty-based waiver) the applicable tax treaty article.
    • R102-R: evidence that the employee meets the treaty exemption conditions (days of presence in Canada, employer residency, attribution of pay).
    • T1287 / T1288: details of the acting services contract and estimated Canadian expenses related to earning the income.
    • Regulation 805.1 payee certificate: details about the permanent establishment in Canada, the nature and amount of the payments, and evidence that the payments are attributable to the permanent establishment.

  4. Submit your application

    Submit the waiver application at least 30 days before the services begin in Canada.

    Online

    In your CRA account, you and your representatives can submit waiver and tax reduction applications.

    CRA sign in

    By mail

    Send completed waiver and certification applications to:

    International Tax Services Office
    Canada Revenue Agency
    Post Office Box 9769, Station T
    Ottawa ON  K1G 3Y4
    Canada

  5. Wait for CRA's written authorization

    The CRA aims to process waiver applications within 30 business days.

    If the CRA approves your application, it sends a letter to both the non-resident and the payer. The letter confirms the approved reduced rate and the period it covers.

    Important: You must not apply a reduced withholding rate or tax reduction before you receive written authorization from the CRA.

  6. Apply the reduced rate

    Once you receive the CRA's authorization letter, withhold at the approved rate for the period covered by the waiver. Reporting requirements differ by form type.

    Reporting requirements after CRA authorization
    Form type Report on
    R105 (Regulation 105 waiver) T4A-NR slip
    R102-R (Regulation 102 waiver) T4 slip via payroll
    T1287 / T1288 (film and video acting) NR4 slip using exemption code "J"
    Simplified waiver for artists and athletes NR4 slip at the treaty rate

    Secondary withholding obligations

    Even if the CRA approves a waiver, the non-resident is not relieved of their own obligation to withhold and remit tax on payments they make to sub-contractors or employees for services provided in Canada.

If you don't receive authorization

Continue withholding at the standard Regulation 105 or Regulation 102 rate. The non-resident can later file a Canadian income tax return to claim a refund of any tax withheld in excess of their actual tax liability.
