Withholding tax on services provided in Canada

  • Who has to withhold tax
  • How much to withhold
  • Apply for a waiver or reduction
  • Remitting and reporting obligations

How much to withhold

The amount you must withhold depends on whether the non-resident is a contractor or an employee.

  • Withholding for contractors
  • Withholding for employees
  • Director's fees
  • Tax treaty exemptions
  • Penalties for not withholding

Withholding for contractors

You must withhold 15% of any fees, commissions, or other amounts you pay to a non-resident for services provided in Canada. This applies to payments for independent contractor services, not employment.

The 15% rate applies to the gross amount of the payment. Do not subtract any expenses or deductions before calculating the withholding.

You must withhold and remit this tax even if:

  • the non-resident has not invoiced you for the tax
  • the non-resident claims a treaty exemption (unless the CRA has approved a waiver)
  • you are also a non-resident of Canada

When to remit

Under Regulation 108, you must remit the withheld tax to the CRA by the 15th day of the month after the month you made the payment. For example, if you make a payment on March 20, you must remit the withheld tax by April 15.

Withholding for employees

If the non-resident is your employee (not a contractor), you must withhold income tax at a graduated tax rate, Canada Pension Plan (CPP) contributions, and Employment Insurance (EI) premiums from their salary or wages. This is the same as what you would do for a Canadian resident employee.

Use the CRA's Payroll Deductions Online Calculator or the payroll deduction tables to determine the correct amounts to withhold.

You must register for a payroll account with the CRA and remit the deductions on the same schedule as your other payroll remittances.

Director's fees

Fees paid to a non-resident director of a Canadian corporation are generally subject to Regulation 102 withholding at graduated payroll rates, not the flat 15% Regulation 105 rate.

You must:

  • deduct income tax at graduated rates from the director's fees
  • report the fees on a T4 slip, not a T4A-NR slip
  • remit the deductions with your regular payroll remittances

In some situations, Regulation 105 may apply instead. If you are unsure which applies to your situation, contact the CRA's International Tax Services Office.

Tax treaty exemptions

Canada has tax treaties with many countries. If the non-resident is eligible under the treaty-based waiver guidelines, they may qualify for a full exemption from Regulation 105 withholding. If they are not eligible, or there is no applicable treaty, no exemption applies and you must withhold at the standard rate.

You must not stop withholding on your own. A treaty exemption applies only after we have authorized it in writing. There are two ways a treaty-based exemption can be obtained:

  1. Approved waiver: The non-resident applies for a waiver using Form R105. If we approve, you will receive a letter confirming the exemption. Only then can you stop withholding.
  2. Tax return refund: You withhold at the standard 15% rate. The non-resident files a Canadian income tax return and claims a refund of any tax withheld that they were not liable for under the treaty.

If you stop withholding without our waiver authorization and the non-resident does not qualify for a treaty exemption, you may be liable for the shortfall plus interest and penalties.

Penalties for not withholding

If you fail to withhold, remit, or report as required, you may face consequences under section 227 of the Income Tax Act, including:

  • Penalty: 10% of the amount you should have withheld, or 20% for repeat failures within the same calendar year
  • Interest: compound daily interest on the overdue amount from the date it was due
  • Assessment: the CRA may assess you for the full amount of tax you should have withheld
