Withholding tax on services provided in Canada

Non-resident payers

If you are a non-resident with employees or contractors providing services in Canada, you have Canadian withholding obligations. Your obligations apply regardless of where your business is based.

The rules depend on whether the non-residents you pay are contractors or employees.

If you pay non-resident contractors

If you pay fees, commissions, or other amounts to a non-resident contractor for services provided in Canada, you must withhold 15% Regulation 105 tax. This obligation applies whether you are a resident or non-resident of Canada.

You must withhold even if the non-resident is from a country that has a tax treaty with Canada. A treaty may exempt the income, but it does not remove your duty to withhold unless we have approved a waiver.

Payments covered

Under paragraph 153(1)(g) of the Income Tax Act and Regulation 105, you must withhold tax on:

  • fees for professional services (such as consulting, legal, accounting, or engineering)
  • commissions
  • management or administration fees
  • technical service fees
  • payments for artistic or athletic performances
  • payments for acting services in film or video productions

For our full guidance, see IC75-6, Required withholding from amounts paid to non-residents providing services in Canada.

When services are considered to be in Canada

Services are considered to be provided in Canada when any part of the work is physically performed in Canada. This applies even if:

  • the contract was signed outside Canada
  • the non-resident's head office is outside Canada
  • the payment is made outside Canada
  • the final product or deliverable is used outside Canada

If a non-resident provides services partly in Canada and partly outside Canada, you only withhold on the portion of the payment for services provided in Canada. You must be able to support the allocation with documentation, such as time records or the terms of the contract.

Exceptions to withholding

You do not need to withhold Regulation 105 tax when:

  • we have approved a waiver or reduction of withholding for the non-resident (see Apply for a waiver or reduction)
  • you are reimbursing reasonable travel or living expenses the non-resident incurred while providing services in Canada
  • the payment is for goods only, with no service component

Even if you believe a treaty exemption applies, you must still withhold unless we have approved a waiver. The non-resident can file a Canadian income tax return to claim a refund of any tax withheld in excess of their actual tax liability.

If you have non-resident employees

If you have employees working in Canada, you must withhold and remit income tax at a graduated rate, Canada Pension Plan (CPP) contributions, and Employment Insurance (EI) premiums from their pay. This requirement applies even if your business is based outside Canada. Regulation 102 applies to any employer, resident or non-resident of Canada, whose employees work in Canada. For how to calculate these deductions and when to remit them, see T4001, Employers' Guide – Payroll Deductions and Remittances.

You must register for a payroll account with us. You can do this by completing Form RC1, Request for a Business Number, or by calling our Business Window at 1-800-959-5525.

You must withhold even if your employees qualify for a tax treaty exemption. A treaty does not remove your duty to withhold unless we have authorized relief. Two relief mechanisms are available for Regulation 102: a waiver application using Form R102-R for an individual employee (see Apply for a waiver or reduction), or the non-resident employer certification program for your business.

Director's fees

Director's fees paid to non-resident directors of Canadian corporations are subject to Regulation 102 withholding (payroll deductions at a graduated rate), the same as other employee pay. A director holds an "office" under the Income Tax Act, which means they are treated as an employee for withholding purposes. You report director's fees on a T4 slip, not a T4A-NR.

If you are unsure how to treat a director's payment, contact us.

Non-resident employer certification program

The non-resident employer certification program lets qualifying non-resident employers pay their employees who work in Canada without withholding Canadian income tax. If you are certified, you do not need to withhold income tax from the pay of qualifying employees.

You may still need to withhold and remit CPP contributions and EI premiums, depending on the circumstances.

Online submission available

In your CRA account, you and your representatives can submit your request for non-resident employer certification (RC473).

Qualifying for certification

To qualify for certification, you must meet all of the following conditions:

  • you are a resident of a country that has a tax treaty with Canada
  • you do not have a permanent establishment in Canada (within the meaning of the applicable tax treaty)

Each employee you want to include must also meet all of the following conditions:

  • the employee is a resident of a country that has a tax treaty with Canada
  • the employee is exempt from Canadian tax on their employment income under the terms of the treaty
  • the employee works in Canada for fewer than 45 days in the calendar year, or is present in Canada for fewer than 90 days in any 12-month period that includes the time of the payment
Difference between days worked and days of presence

Days worked in Canada include only the days during which the employee is physically present in Canada and paid by the employer for time spent in Canada. This generally excludes weekends, days off, and holidays.

Days of presence in Canada include every day the employee is physically present in Canada, including weekends, holidays, and vacation days spent in Canada.

How to apply

To apply for certification, complete Form RC473, Application for Non-Resident Employer Certification.

You need to include the following information:

  • your business number (BN) or request one using Form RC1
  • details about each employee who will work in Canada
  • the dates and location of work in Canada
  • the applicable tax treaty article that provides the exemption

Submit the application to the International Tax Services Office, either online through your CRA account or by mail.

CRA sign in

The certification is valid for 2 calendar years.

After you are certified

Once certified, you must:

  • keep track of the number of days each qualifying employee works in or is present in Canada
  • notify the CRA if an employee no longer meets the conditions (for example, if they exceed the 45-day or 90-day limit)
  • begin withholding income tax from that employee's pay immediately if they no longer qualify
  • file an annual T4 information return for all employees who worked in Canada, even if no tax was withheld

Certification may be revoked if you do not withhold as required

If you fail to withhold and remit as required, the CRA may revoke your certification and assess you for the tax that should have been withheld, plus penalties and interest.

Employees attending conferences in Canada

If you are a non-resident employer and your employees attend a conference or convention in Canada, special rules may apply.

Non-resident employees who live in a treaty country and attend a conference in Canada for a short period may be exempt from Canadian tax under the treaty. The conditions are generally the same as those for the certification program:

  • the employee is a resident of a treaty country
  • the employee's presence in Canada is fewer than 45 days in the calendar year (or 90 days in any 12-month period)
  • the employer does not have a permanent establishment in Canada
  • the employee's pay is not borne by a permanent establishment in Canada

If these conditions are met, you may not need to withhold income tax from their pay for the conference period. However, you should apply for non-resident employer certification to confirm your eligibility.
